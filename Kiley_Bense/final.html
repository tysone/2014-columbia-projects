and

By Kiley Bense

I
t’s a gold class ring, Roman Catholic High 1905, with three blue letters engraved on a beveled top: BHH. My mother put it on in a hospital room in 1998. By then the blue was nearly gone. I remember seeing the ring on her finger. She told me that it really belonged to my little brother, because his name was Brendan.

Seventy years ago, Brendan Hynes Halvey, my grandmother’s father, sat down at his desk in his house at 4850 Germantown Avenue, Philadelphia, PA to write a letter. “My dear Helen—” he began, and maybe then he stopped, and thought, or paused to consider the weight of that Tuesday and the one hundred heavy days that had gone before it. “This morning I put Bob on a train at North Philadelphia to return to camp,” he wrote, “and the memory of a like trip there, last July, to start another soldier on the first lap of a one-way journey, has had me ‘down’ all day.”

Bob was Brendan’s oldest son, and the soldier who never came back was his youngest son and my grandmother’s little brother. Dick Halvey—the handsome kid who’d been a star basketball player at Roman Catholic High, who’d written his class’s valedictorian speech, who was all of 21 when he enlisted—that kid—was dead. One hundred days after Dick died in a hospital in North Africa, his father sent a letter to his sister Helen, and Bob got on a train in Philadelphia bound for camp.

Philadelphia, 1941. Dick Halvey on the front porch.
I decided to go to Arlington Cemetery in July because of a photograph that was taken there in 1963: three boys with their parents and grandfather standing in a tight knot around a white gravestone. One of those kids is my dad, at 7, in knee socks and shorts and wedged between his parents, his head covered in that downy fuzz of little-boy buzzcuts, with ears like open shutters. It must’ve been summer, then, too. Taps were playing and my grandmother cried.

F
or months I have gone absolutely no place except Regent Street. I tried the Movies but the effect was not at all desirable. I know in the past I have been accused of many things by many people—not that the charges ever worried me—but only of late can anyone say that I am absolutely licked.

The metro platform at Arlington is crowded. People are wearing shirts emblazoned with eagles’ heads and “Don’t Tread on Me,” wearing ball caps with Army logos and dangling flag-shaped keychains. There are also people in visors and flip-flops, holding maps and talking too loudly. The woman at the information desk says that it’ll be $8 to take a tram to section 34, and would I like an audio guide?

We know from Dick’s letters he was under fire from the day before he landed until he died; we know he served Mass daily either at a base or in the field where instead of a cassock he was ordered to wear his knife and revolver, but through it all not a word of complaint, simply a reminder that someday we would all be together again.

Walking the two miles out to section 34 isn’t so bad: just take Eisenhower Drive to Bradley and turn at Pershing. It’s July in Virginia but mercifully it doesn’t feel like it. Clouds—the forecast said Evening Thunderstorms—linger, and the air is thick. The grass hums with what must be the sounds of thousands of mosquitos’ wings. When I get to the crest of the hill where the World War II veterans lie, I have to pull out the scrap of paper again that I scribbled the number of the grave on: 2162.

A
rlington is supposed to be a sanctuary, and out here, where the wind is hushed and the tourists don’t go, it does seem sacred. What’s not so hallowed are the numbers, the infinite numbers and their stones, so many rows that they touch the horizon. When I look at the rows, there’s only one word on my tongue: Why? There are many soldiers buried here whose lives weren’t stopped forever in the middle of a frame, who weren’ t killed in battle. But there are far too many who were. One is too many.

After Dick died, my grandmother, Patricia, filled a green leather album with all the small consolations of loss: the obituaries, the notes, her brother's identification card; it’s where I found the picture of Arlington, and Brendan’s letter. It is too obvious to say that she never got over it, too easy to spin her story into an unbroken line that leads directly from sorrow and an empty house to the Valiums she popped into her children’s mouths and her own, the window she threatened to throw herself from when my aunt got engaged to a Protestant, the daily ironing she wept over. I barely knew my grandmother but I think that there was a kind of wild sadness crouched somewhere deep inside her chest; that it probably never let her go.

D
ick’s stone is midway up the hill, under the shade of a cherry tree in bloom. The tree’s roots have erupted around the stone next to his so that a whole corner of it is submerged under soil and knotted bark. What I wonder is this: how many years has it been since someone else stood here? How many since anyone came here for him? Since a girl sat on this grass and cried over him? Why didn’t I remember to bring flowers? Should I snap a branch from the tree and lay it where his head might be?

I feel I have paid a big price these past months, and also realize that such things happen for the best. If I am to be called upon to give another in this great cause I hope God sends me strength enough to face it.

I don’t believe in heaven, or God. All those Sundays I spent in church as a kid, the Hail Marys I recited and the rosaries I counted: none of it meant anything to me. I don’t believe that my great-uncle knows that I visited him or that the deeds of the living matter to the dead. But I do believe that coming here was important, somehow. That when I walk down Spring Garden it is important to know that my grandmother grieved on this street, to remember that my great-grandfather was standing at the doorstep of number 5523 on a Saturday morning in April when he got a telegram that fell out of his hands.

I
t’s important because I carry my grandmother with me—her oil painting and her burdens and her birthstone; because I’ve got some amalgam of my great-grandfather’s stubbornness and silences in my heart, because I have my mom’s ambition and my dad’s blue eyes. Whether more or less than the sum of their parts, I’m still made up of them.

Roman Catholic High endures on the corner of Broad and Vine, the hulking stone building that Brendan fled to on an afternoon in 1903, desperate to enroll after St. Joe’s Prep expelled him for failing Greek. He never forgave the Jesuits for kicking him out, but he had enough pride in his Roman degree that he sent both his sons there. Enough pride that on his deathbed he chose his class ring as a parting gift to his only daughter Patricia, enough that on hers, she gave it to her daughter-in-law with the understanding that it would someday be worn by Brendan’s namesake, a little boy who was born on Patricia’s birthday in 1994.

A
t 19, my brother is still too young to wear Brendan’s ring. Maybe he never will. He does not know (or chooses not to remember) the stories; has never seen the black and white snapshot of Dick, in uniform and grinning, or pored over the letters Dick sent home from the front, lopsided sentences written in pencil on unlined paper. But maybe it doesn’t matter that my brother has forgotten the ring’s existence and the things it has seen—so long as one of us never does.

That He allows me to live long enough to repay the great kindnesses of my many friends, but not long enough to forget any of them, is all I ask.
