80 & Approaching:
Growing Old in Hamilton Heights

By Emily Martinez

Well, you have
to be strong.

Juana Cepeda, 79

She's well-cared for by family and has a home attendant, but she forgets. Like she forgets my name and my mother's. It's funny, Tersidad said, because when she was well she never wanted to visit. La memoria responded my grandfather, it's gone.

Moro de gandules,
unos tostonsitos, aguacate...

Euclides Nunez, 76

Some of his favorite foods. If Juana and Euclides seem to look alike, it's because they're siblings. They live a block away from one another, and they both have Alzheimer's. They hardly see each other. When I showed her a photo of her brother my grandmother said, "Isn't he dead?" No, that's another brother. Delighted, she asked, How did he look? Gordito? Yes.

Hoping for health, even a little bit.

Tersidad Nunez, 71

Any hopes for this year? Their son Joseluis gives them a hint. La salud, Mami. He's recovering from heart surgery, and has just been diagnosed with skin cancer. He inherited both diseases from his mother. Tersidad, who used to be a home attendant, cares for her husband and son.

Education
will carry
you forward.

Miguel Nunez, 78

Behind him, his daughter's Bachelor's degree from Tufts University. Nearly all of his children have degrees.

I'm always
among my family.

Francisco De Garcia, 80

I'm a homebody, he said, I like tranquility.
