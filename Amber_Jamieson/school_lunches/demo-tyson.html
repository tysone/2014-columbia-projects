No such thing as a free lunch

New York City's new mayor, Bill de Blasio, came to power promising to make school lunches free for all public school children.

De Blasio's executive city budget, released on May 9, did not include the free school lunch program, but the mayor says he hopes to implement it in the future.

But is?

Around 780,000 of the 1.1 million children currently enrolled in city schools qualify for a free or reduced-price lunch (which costs parents $0.70) due to their family's incomes, but only 530,000 of those children are enrolled in the program. One of the reasons, says the City Council, who is pushing hard for universal meals, is the stigma attached to free lunches.

For years, they have been dubbed "free-free", meaning some children avoid them because of teasing from other children.

The most common hot food served in New York City public schools is cheese pizza. Pizza counts as a grain and as a meat alternative, as the melted cheese is protein. The pizza is divisive, with Karina Heath, a 15-year-old from Brooklyn, saying she's sick of being fed it constantly.

Each menu, made monthly by the Department of Education, includes five components: a meat component or its alternative, fruit, vegetable, grain and milk. A child must choose at least three of these.
