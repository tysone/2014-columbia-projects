Being Brielle

Brielle, 25, is one of New York 's 23,800 transgender people of who tend to experience acute levels of discrimination and economic insecurity that has devastating effects, particularly on the lives of transgender women of color. Time Square, New York. November 29, 2013.

Though LGBT rights has won landmark legislative victories in their quest for equal citizenship rights, public perception of transgender people is lagging severely behind. Time Square, New York,November 29, 2013.

Many transgender women of color, like Brielle, are socially excluded and endure huge disparities in accessing, employment, healthcare, and accommodation, due to pervasive discrimination. Time Square, New York. November 29, 2013.

Everyday activities like riding the subway, walking the street, or going into a store can be a great source of stress for Brielle due to harassment, nasty comments, and hostile stares. Time Square, New York. 2014.

Valerie, 22, and Brielle, 25, in their apartment on Tinton Avenue, South Bronx. Though the underlying presence of negative statistics haunts their lives. They often speak in terms of optimism, hope, and dreams of the future. South Bronx, New York, 2014.

"I do sex work because it's fun. I'm someone who likes adrenaline, excitement, I like to be someone outside the box looking in, I like the attention," said Valerie. South Bronx, New York, 2014

LGBT minorities have remained largely invisible in mainstream economy, media and culture. Unemployment rates of transgender women of color are four times the national average. Many in this demographic rely on sex work as a source of income. South Bronx, New York, 2014.

Transgender women of color are four times more likely to live in extreme poverty, with a household income of less than $10,000. Valerie, 22, and Tara, 22, in the kitchen of thier Tinton Avenue apartment. South Bronx, New York, 2014.

Dolores Nettles, mother of Islan Nettles a 21 year old African American transgender woman murdered in Harlem, at a demonstration protesting the NYPD's negligence in the aftermath of the crime. In 2013, it is estimated that 238 transgender people were murdered through hate crimes, worldwide. 16 people were killed in the US. Police Plaza, Manhattan, New York, 2014.
