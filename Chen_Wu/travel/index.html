10 Places to See Wild Life This Summer
By Chen Wu
Haven’t decided where to go this summer? Love animals in the wild? This list may help you out! Here are 10 places in the world that are suitable and popular to see wild life this summer.

Background photo: Yellowstone National Park, stevetulk/flicker
1
Yellowstone National Park, United States

Yellowstone National Park, which was inaugurated in 1872, has always been a hot destination to spend summer. It is one of the world’s most famous national park and it has the largest concentration of watchable wildlife in the lower 48 states in the United States. Depending on what type of wildlife you want to see, you’ll have better luck finding it in certain parts of the Park. Its wildlife including moose, bison, the great grizzly bear, wolves and other almost 60 mammals. The park offers a countless number of outdoor adventures, including hiking, snowmobiling, fly fishing and camping. There are over 2,000 campsites for travelers, as well as a number of lodges. You can also visit the Grand Teton National Park, which is located 10 miles south of Yellowston, during your trip.

2
Galàpagos Islands, Ecuador

Located 600 miles off the western coast of Ecuador, the Galàpagos Islands are of volcanic origin and have never been connected with the mainland. Therefore, species there all have abilities to fly, swin or float and larger mammals which reside at the top of the food chain are usually not able to go to the islands. Thus, with a lack of predators in the Galàpagos, many of the animals there appear tame and do not fear humans. You can see Marine Iguana, the only iguana adapted to life in the water, over 150-year-old Galàpagos Giant Tortoise and many other rare animals. You can also see many species of birds including Darwin’s finches, Blue-footed Boobies, Frigatebirds, Albatrosses, Galàpagos Penguins, and Flightless Cormorants.

3
Amazon Rainforest, South America

Amazon Rainforest is home to many more species of plants and animals than any other terrestrial ecosystem on the planet. One-third of all species in the world are thought to reside in the Amazon Rainforest. It is home to reptiles, amphibians, primates, tapirs, capybaras, and jaguars, you will meet countless wildlife during your journey. It has 3,000 freshwater fish species, much more more that what has been counted in the entire Atlantic Ocean. Amazon Rainforest also contains over 4,000 species of butterflies. In the river itself you may spot a manatee or the Amazon River dolphin (also known as the pink river dolphin or boto). Since the diversity of wildlife in the Amazon region is truly staggering, the region is definitely worth visiting.

4
Alaska, United States

known as the Great Land, Alaska is home to both land and sea species wildlife. With the largest state park system in America and 65 percent of the state being managed by the Federal government (including its 15 national parks), Alaska is an unspoiled safe haven for wildlife ranging from bald eagles to beluga whales. Even in Anchorage, Alaska’s largest town of 300,000 residents, you’re likely to spot one of the 1,000 moose that call the city home. You can see land animals including black, grizzly, and polar bears, moose, caribou, mountain goats, Dall sheep, foxes, and coyotes. Off the coast, you can spot Humpback and Killer whales, dolphins, sea otters, seals, sea lions, bald eagles, and many types of bird including puffins, kittiwakes, and oystercatchers.

5
Foping National Nature Reserve, China

Do you want to catch a glimpse of wild pandas? Then you should travel to the Foping National Nature Reserve in Shaanxi Province. The remote bamboo forests of this nature reserve are the home to the world’s highest density of giant pandas. Though seeing one means spending a small fortune to take a week-long trek through the reserve, the scenery and potential payoff make it worthwhile. Nestled in the bio-diverse Qinling Mountains, the reserve is surrounded by other sanctuaries, like the Zhouzhi Nature Reserve, which is in the foothills of the Qinling Mountains and specializes in rescuing injured animals and protecting endangered species, including giant pandas and golden monkeys.

6
Shark Bay, Western Australia

There are very few places in the world where you can see wild dolphins so easily as in Shark Bay, a World Heritage Site in the Gascoyne region of Western Australia. Divided by shallow banks, the bay has many peninsulas and islands. It’s vast sea grass beds are full of turtles, dugongs, rays and seasnakes and the primordial stromatolites at Hamelin Pool are living descendents of the earliest known plant forms. It is home to about 10,000 dugongs and over 325 fish species, with many sharks and rays. There are many Indo-Pacific bottlenose dolphins, particularly at Monkey Mia. The area supports 26 threatened Australian mammal species, over 230 species of bird, and nearly 150 species of reptile. It is an important breeding and nursery ground for fish, crustaceans, and coelenterates.

7
Kalahari Desert, Namibia

The Kalahari Desert is a large semi-arid sandy savannah in southern Africa, extending 900,000 square kilometres, covering much of Botswana and parts of Namibia and South Africa. As a semi-desert with huge tracts of excellent grazing after good rains, the Kalahari supports more animals and plants than a true desert like the Namib Desert to the west. With small amounts of rainfall and high summer temperature, the Kalahari is home to many migratory birds and animals. Previously havens for wild animals from elephants to giraffes, and for predators such as lions and cheetahs, the riverbeds are now mostly grazing spots, though leopards and cheetahs can still be found. The area is now heavily grazed and cattle fences restrict the movement of wildlife.

8
Kenya, Africa

Kenya is home to an amazing array of wildlife, especially the famous "The Big Five", including the lion, elephant, buffalo, rhinoceros (white and black), and leopard, a very elusive animal, hunting at night and spending its days resting in trees. Of course many other types of animals can also be found all around Kenya including zebras, giraffes, cheetahs, jackals, oryx, hyenas, baboons, monkeys, kudus, gazelle, antelope, hippos, wildebeest and more. The most popular wildlife park in Kenya, the Masai Mara Game Reserve is where you can witness the incredible migration of millions of zebra and wildebeest from July through October.

9
Madagascar, Africa

Madagascar is sometimes referred to as the ’8th continent’ in recognition of the island’s unique and diverse fauna. Located roughly 400 kilometers west of the African mainland, Madagascar has been a world unto itself for more than 80 million years. The island’s wildlife has evolved in a way that is almost entirely unique to the island. With almost 600,000 square kilometers, Madagascar is the world’s fourth largest island. Covered in rainforests, its coast is lined with tropical beaches. In addition to its land–dwelling animals such as lemurs, the one type of animal that can only be found in Madagascar, and sloths, Madagascar also has an offshore coral reef and an abundant marine life. Because of its colonial history, the easiest way to reach Madagascar is not from Africa, but via plane from France. Flights depart regularly from Paris to its capital, Antananarivo.

10
Antarctica

As a continent of extremes, Antarctica holds the title of coldest, driest, and windiest location on earth. It’s surprising that wildlife can thrive in such extreme temperatures and climate. Seals, whales, penguins and a large diversity of seabirds migrate to Antarctica to breed and feed on the endless supply of krill, fish, and crustaceans. Travelers to this frozen land are rewarded for their persistence with stunning scenery, unique wildlife and a chance to visit a place that few ever get to see. The most popular way to get there is to take a cruise from South America. The ships depart regularly during summer from Ushuaia and Tierra Del Fuego in Patagonia. Less frequent ships also depart from Buenos Aires, Tasmania and New Zealand.
